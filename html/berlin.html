Welcome to The Pergamon Museum

The Pergamon Museum, located in Berlin, Germany, is one of the world’s most famous museums for ancient art and archaeology. It houses grand historical monuments like the Pergamon Altar, the Ishtar Gate of Babylon, and the Market Gate of Miletus.

Ancient Wonders on Display

Gold Coin – Florentine Double Portrait Florin

Origin: Florence, Italy, c. 15th century CE.This rare gold coin features facing busts of two crowned rulers—symbolizing power, unity, or dynastic marriage. The inscriptions and style link it to Renaissance Florence, a center of art, banking, and politics. Coins like this were not only currency but also tools of propaganda, showing strength and legitimacy of ruling families.

Islamic Architectural Tiles and Calligraphy

Origin: Persia or Central Asia, 13th–15th century.These beautiful ceramic tiles showcase intricate Islamic calligraphy and arabesque floral designs. The fragment and hexagonal plaque use Kufic and Naskh script styles, often featuring verses from the Qur’an or poetic inscriptions. Tiles like these decorated mosques, palaces, and tombs, merging spiritual messages with visual beauty in Islamic art.

Roman Marble Bust of Caracalla

Origin: Roman Empire, early 3rd century CE.This lifelike marble bust represents Emperor Caracalla, known for his military strength and harsh rule. His intense expression, furrowed brow, and short beard reflect Roman sculptural realism and political power. Busts like these were displayed in public and private spaces to promote imperial authority and presence.

Classical Greek Temple Architecture Drawing

Origin: Ancient Greece, Classical Period.This detailed architectural drawing shows the structure of a Greek temple, likely inspired by the Parthenon or Temple of Hephaestus. Greek temples featured Doric or Ionic columns, a triangular pediment with mythological sculptures, and harmonious proportions. These temples were dedicated to gods and were central to civic and spiritual life in ancient Greek cities.

The Persian Archers – Processional Frieze from Susa

Origin: Achaemenid Empire, c. 5th century BCE.This glazed brick relief depicts Persian warriors in ceremonial attire, possibly the "Immortals," the elite royal guard of the Achaemenid Empire. It was found in the ancient city of Susa (modern Iran), once one of the empire’s capitals. The vibrant colors, detailed robes, and repeated stance reflect Persian ideals of order, power, and royal decorum. This frieze adorned palace walls and demonstrated the might and discipline of the empire.

Mesopotamian Sculptures and Inlayed Columns

Origin: Sumer, Akkad, or early Mesopotamia, c. 3000–2000 BCE\.This exhibit includes limestone and bronze artifacts such as lion heads (possibly from ceremonial furniture), votive figurines, and beautifully inlaid columns using shell and black limestone. These items reflect the religious and artistic craftsmanship of early Mesopotamian city-states. Temples were adorned with such decorative pieces to honor deities, showing a blend of power, faith, and beauty in early civilization

Mesopotamian Cylinder Seals and Impressions

Origin: Sumer, Akkad, Babylon (c. 3000–1000 BCE).Cylinder seals were small carved stones rolled over clay to leave detailed impressions. They served as personal signatures or identification tools in ancient Mesopotamia. Each seal often depicted gods, royal figures, and scenes of worship or daily life. These seals were worn as amulets and used in contracts, reflecting both artistic skill and administrative innovation in early civilizations.

Diana the Huntress Automaton (Mechanical Table Centerpiece)

Origin: Germany, c. 16th century CE Material: Gilded silver, enamel, jewels, and mechanical clockwork Description & History: This intricate silver-gilt automaton features Diana, the Roman goddess of the hunt, elegantly seated atop a majestic stag. Around her are hunting dogs, with detailed floral and jewel decorations. The piece is not just artistic—it’s mechanical. Created during the Renaissance, it was designed to move across the table during feasts, with wheels hidden underneath and sometimes music or moving parts inside. Such automatons were popular in royal courts of Germany and the Holy Roman Empire. They showcased wealth, technological innovation, and mythological themes.
