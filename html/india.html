Welcome to The India Museum

The India Museum is one of the oldest and most famous museums in the country. It holds a large collection of ancient coins, sculptures, paintings, weapons, and beautiful art from different parts of India. From the Indus Valley to the Mughal Empire and beyond, the museum tells the story of India’s rich and diverse past. It’s a great place to learn about India’s heritage and traditions in one visit.

Ancient Wonders on Display

Sunga Period Narrative Pottery (Terracotta Relief)

This large terracotta vessel is carved in high relief with dynamic scenes of warriors, animals, and ritual processions. Likely dating to the Sunga dynasty (2nd–1st century BCE), it was probably used in ceremonial contexts or placed in temples. The storytelling style resembles early Indian epics like the Ramayana or Mahabharata. The top features floral patterns while the middle showcases intense action scenes, perhaps of a battle or royal hunt. Such pots not only served practical purposes but also stood as a medium for visual storytelling, helping preserve cultural myths and heroic tales.

Lion Capital of Ashoka

This sculpture is the Lion Capital of Ashoka, from Sarnath, India, dating back to the 3rd century BCE. It was erected by Emperor Ashoka of the Maurya dynasty and is now the national emblem of India. It consists of four Asiatic lions standing back to back, symbolizing power, courage, and confidence, and sits atop a base with Dharma Chakras (wheel of law).The Lion Capital is a pillar sculpture originally erected by Emperor Ashoka in the 3rd century BCE at Sarnath, marking the site of Buddha’s first sermon. It features four lions standing back-to-back, symbolizing vigilance, power, courage, and pride.

Statue of an Indian Water Carrier Woman

This sculpture represents a traditional Indian woman carrying a water pot on her head, symbolizing rural Indian life. Such statues became prominent during the colonial period, particularly in 19th-century British India, where European artists and patrons admired the grace of Indian village life. The detailed attire, jewelry, and posture highlight regional craftsmanship and celebrate feminine strength and cultural heritage.This bronze sculpture beautifully captures a traditional Indian village woman in a graceful pose carrying a pot on her head, an everyday scene across rural India. The woman is adorned in a saree with intricate draping, detailed jewelry, and a serene facial expression, reflecting the strength and elegance of Indian womanhood.

Head of a Goddess (Possibly Durga or a Yakshi)

This sculpture shows a crowned female head with a halo and lions beside her, suggesting Durga or a Yakshi, both revered female figures in Hindu and Buddhist art. This artifact likely comes from Mathura or Central India during the Gupta period (4th–6th century CE), known for idealized facial features and spiritual expression.This sculpture fragment, featuring a serene female face with a crown and halo, likely represents a Yakshi, Durga, or a celestial attendant. The intricate headdress and stylized lions suggest Gupta or post-Gupta period influences (4th–6th century CE), a time known for idealized and spiritualized human figures.

Ornate Ritual Conch (Shankha)

This is a heavily ornamented shankha (conch shell), used in Hindu rituals. The shankha is associated with Lord Vishnu and is blown during worship to purify the surroundings. This particular conch has metal casing with detailed carvings, showing the combination of utility and sacred design. It likely dates back to medieval South India and was used in temple ceremonies.The conch shell, or shankha, has deep spiritual significance in Hinduism. Used in religious ceremonies and temple rituals, the blowing of the conch is believed to purify the environment and drive away evil spirits.

Elephant-Headed Bird Figurine

This is a zoomorphic sculpture combining features of an elephant and a bird. Such imaginative hybrid forms are common in Indian folk and tribal art, often representing mythological creatures or symbolizing harmony in nature. Likely from a decorative or ritual context, this piece reflects the rich storytelling traditions and spiritual symbolism in Indian artistry.This unique artifact is a small statue representing a mythical creature with the body of a bird and the head of an elephant. Such hybrid forms are prominent in Indian folklore, tribal traditions, and temple iconography, where they often serve as symbols of divine balance or the unity of opposites.

Torso of a Female Deity

This bronze torso, adorned with detailed jewelry, is likely part of a larger sculpture of a Hindu goddess such as Parvati or Lakshmi. It reflects the Chola dynasty’s exquisite bronze casting tradition from Tamil Nadu, which flourished between the 9th and 13th centuries. These pieces were often used in temples and processions.This finely sculpted bronze torso represents a goddess, most likely Parvati, Lakshmi, or another divine feminine form from Hindu tradition. The craftsmanship, ornamentation, and style point to the famed Chola dynasty bronzes of Tamil Nadu (9th–13th century CE).

Bejeweled Dagger (Khanjar)

This jeweled dagger is known as a khanjar, a ceremonial weapon of royalty in Mughal India. Decorated with gold, rubies, emeralds, and diamonds, such daggers were symbols of honor and valor. They were also exchanged as gifts among nobles and kept as heirlooms in royal treasuries.This luxurious dagger, encrusted with rubies, emeralds, and pearls, is a fine example of Mughal weaponry and decorative art from the 17th or 18th century. Known as a khanjar, its curved blade and ornate hilt made it a symbol of power, status, and martial prowess.

Katar with Chakram (Punch Dagger with Throwing Weapon)

This is a katar, a traditional Indian push dagger, with an attached chakram, a circular throwing weapon. This rare combination suggests either a ceremonial piece or a high-status warrior’s weapon, showcasing both Indian metallurgical skill and martial innovation, likely from Rajput or Sikh traditions.This rare weapon is a katar, or push dagger, with an attached chakram, a circular throwing weapon used by warriors, especially Rajputs and Sikhs. This dual-purpose weapon could be used in close combat and for ranged attacks.

Necklace from Mughal India

In 17th century CE Origin: Varanasi, India. This magnificent necklace from the Mughal era is adorned with diamonds, emeralds, pearls, and gold. Mughal jewelry was renowned for its opulence, intricate design, and symbolic use of gemstones. These pieces were not just adornments but also conveyed power, status, and cultural refinement. This one is preserved in the Museum of Islamic Art (MIA), likely in Doha, Qatar.

Mughal Royal Footwear (Juttis)

These are richly embroidered Mughal juttis, made of velvet and embellished with gold thread (zardozi) and beads. Worn by kings and queens, these shoes symbolized luxury and elegance. They reflect the Mughal dynasty's influence on Indian fashion and their appreciation for fine craftsmanship.These elaborately designed juttis are traditional North Indian footwear, popularized during the Mughal era. Made of velvet and decorated with gold thread (zardozi), beads, and sequins, these shoes were worn by royals and nobles for special occasions.
